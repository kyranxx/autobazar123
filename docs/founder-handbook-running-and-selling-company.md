# Founder Handbook: Running And Selling The Company

This is the simple version.

If the other docs feel too technical, start here.

This handbook is meant to teach the basics of:

- how to run the company well
- what to measure
- what to keep organized
- what makes a company easier to sell later

Think of it like a founder's school notebook, not a legal manual.

## The Big Idea

A valuable company is not just:

- a product
- a website
- many users

A valuable company is a machine that:

- attracts demand
- serves customers well
- makes money
- keeps records clean
- can be understood by outsiders

If Autobazar123 works well but is messy inside, a buyer gets nervous.

If Autobazar123 is well run and well documented, the buyer feels safer.

Safer companies usually sell more easily.

## The 5 Jobs Of A Founder

As founder, your job is not only to build product.

Your 5 jobs are:

1. Build something people want.
2. Make sure the company can measure whether it works.
3. Keep the company legally and financially clean.
4. Build repeatable operations, not founder chaos.
5. Keep the company understandable to future investors or buyers.

If you do these 5 things well, you are building a real company, not just a project.

## What "Running The Company Well" Means

In plain language, running the company well means:

- you know what is happening
- you know why it is happening
- you can show the evidence
- the company does not break when one person is absent

That means you need 4 kinds of clarity:

- product clarity
- metric clarity
- financial clarity
- legal clarity

## The 4 Simple Questions You Should Always Be Able To Answer

### 1. Are users getting value?

For Autobazar123:

- are buyers finding ads?
- are sellers getting views and sales?
- are dealers staying because paid ads and premium features help them sell?

### 2. Is the business getting stronger?

- is retention improving?
- is monetization improving?
- is growth efficient?

### 3. Is the company clean and trustworthy?

- do the numbers match?
- do we own the IP?
- are records organized?

### 4. Could someone else take over?

- are key processes documented?
- do accounts belong to the company?
- is knowledge trapped in one founder's head?

If you cannot answer these, the company is harder to run and harder to sell.

## What Makes A Company Easier To Sell

Buyers usually care about these areas:

### Commercial Quality

They want to know:

- do customers really want this?
- is growth real?
- is revenue repeatable?

For Autobazar123, that means:

- sellers pay to post ads
- sellers pay for top / highlight features
- ads get views
- ads get sold
- sellers and dealers come back
- premium products really monetize

Messages and contact actions are useful proof-of-interest, but they are not the business model.

### Data Quality

They want to know:

- can I trust these numbers?
- are the definitions stable?
- do finance and analytics match?

### Legal Quality

They want to know:

- does the company own the code and brand?
- are contracts signed?
- are there hidden disputes or missing approvals?

### Operational Quality

They want to know:

- can the business keep running after the acquisition?
- is the team organized?
- are key systems documented?

### Risk Quality

They want to know:

- is revenue too concentrated?
- is traffic too dependent on one source?
- are privacy or security issues hiding underneath?

## What You Should Do Regularly

This is the part that matters most in practice.

### Every Week

- review top-line traffic and listing views
- review listing quality and moderation issues
- review paid ad and premium feature activity
- review cash and urgent billing issues

Why:
This keeps the company close to reality.

### Every Month

- review paid ads and feature purchases
- review listing views
- review sold listings and time to sale
- review dealer retention
- review dealer revenue retention
- review refunds and churn
- review stale inventory
- review concentration risk
- review cash runway
- write short notes about what changed

Why:
This is how management discipline is built.

### Every Quarter

- clean up contracts and corporate records
- review pricing and packaging
- review key customer concentration
- review vendor list and tool sprawl
- review access control and company-owned accounts
- review roadmap against business goals

Why:
This prevents quiet mess from accumulating.

### Every Year

- review tax and accounting setup
- review cap table and option records
- review IP assignments and contractor paperwork
- review privacy and data retention practices
- review disaster recovery and backup readiness

Why:
This is the kind of work founders skip until diligence, and then regret.

## What To Build First

If you are early, do not try to do everything at once.

Build in this order:

1. Stable IDs for important objects.
2. Metric dictionary.
3. Analytics event spec.
4. Monthly reporting habit.
5. Clean legal and finance records.
6. Data room structure.

This is why:

- stable IDs make tracking possible
- metric definitions make numbers understandable
- event specs make implementation consistent
- reporting creates operating discipline
- legal and finance hygiene makes the company sellable

## What A Buyer Checks During Diligence

Think of diligence as a serious inspection.

The buyer checks whether your story is true.

They usually inspect:

- corporate records
- cap table
- contracts
- employee and contractor documents
- IP ownership
- finance records
- taxes
- product metrics
- customer concentration
- security and privacy
- systems and operations

A buyer is not just asking "Is this company growing?"

They are asking:

- "Is it clean?"
- "Is it real?"
- "Is it sustainable?"
- "Will I inherit hidden problems?"

## What Founders Usually Get Wrong

These are very common mistakes:

- mixing personal and company money
- keeping key accounts in personal ownership
- not signing IP assignment agreements
- not documenting board or shareholder actions
- tracking lots of events but not defining metrics
- counting vanity metrics instead of business metrics
- depending on founder memory instead of systems
- waiting until fundraising or sale to organize records

## The Most Important Habits

If you remember only a few habits, remember these:

### 1. Separate personal and company life

Company money, accounts, domains, and assets should belong to the company.

Why:
This is basic business hygiene and helps preserve trust, accounting clarity, and legal separation.

### 2. Write things down

Write:

- metric definitions
- decisions
- board materials
- contracts
- operational procedures

Why:
Written systems make the business transferable.

### 3. Use one source of truth

For each critical area, know the official source:

- ownership
- revenue
- active dealers
- listing count
- qualified leads

Why:
Conflicting numbers destroy confidence.

### 4. Track outcomes, not just activity

Do not stop at:

- pageviews
- sessions
- raw leads

Track:

- qualified leads
- retention
- revenue quality
- sale outcomes

Why:
Outcomes are what buyers and investors care about.

### 5. Clean up continuously

Do not wait for a deal process to fix:

- contracts
- cap table
- permissions
- missing approvals
- missing invoices

Why:
Fixing these under time pressure is painful and expensive.

## Simple Glossary For Running And Selling A Company

### Board Minutes

Written notes of what the board discussed and approved.

Use case:
If the board approved option grants or a financing, that should appear in minutes or written consents.

### Burn

How much cash the company loses over time.

Use case:
If the company spends EUR30,000 per month and brings in EUR10,000, burn is EUR20,000.

### Cap Table

The ownership table of the company.

Use case:
Shows how much founders, investors, and employees own.

### Cash Runway

How many months the company can survive with current cash and burn.

Use case:
If you have 12 months of runway, you know how much time you have before needing more cash or profitability.

### Churn

The customers or revenue you lose over time.

Use case:
If dealers keep cancelling, churn is rising and the business is weakening.

### Contribution Margin

Revenue left after direct serving costs.

Use case:
Shows whether a customer is economically healthy, not just top-line revenue.

### Corporate Records

The official documents showing how the company has been governed.

Use case:
Includes approvals, minutes, stock issuances, option grants, and major actions.

### Data Room

A structured folder of company documents used during fundraising or sale.

Use case:
Instead of rushing during diligence, you already have organized files ready.

### Due Diligence

The buyer's investigation before buying or investing.

Use case:
They review legal, finance, tech, product, contracts, privacy, and risks.

### Gross Margin

Revenue left after the direct cost of delivering the product or service.

Use case:
Helps show whether growth is high quality or expensive to serve.

### IP Assignment

A legal transfer of created work to the company.

Use case:
Without this, an employee or contractor may still own important code or designs.

### KPI

Key performance indicator.

Use case:
A KPI is an important number used to judge business health, like dealer retention or qualified lead rate.

### Material Contract

A contract important enough that it matters to company value or risk.

Use case:
A major dealer partnership or exclusive distribution deal is usually material.

### Monthly Close

The process of finalizing the company's monthly financial numbers.

Use case:
Makes finance more reliable and easier to explain to investors or buyers.

### Quality Of Earnings

A diligence concept about how sustainable and real the company's earnings are.

Use case:
Buyers want to know whether revenue is recurring and normal, or inflated by one-off items and messy adjustments.

### Revenue Retention

How much revenue remains from the same customer group over time.

Use case:
If the same dealer cohort still pays the same or more later, revenue quality is stronger.

### Source Of Truth

The official place a number or record should come from.

Use case:
The cap table should come from the cap table system, and recognized revenue should come from finance records.

### Stock Option

The right to buy company shares in the future under set rules.

Use case:
Used to compensate employees, but must be tracked and approved properly.

### Take Rate

Platform revenue as a percentage of the value flowing through the marketplace.

Use case:
Relevant if Autobazar123 later moves deeper into transaction flow.

### Valuation

The price or implied worth of the company in a financing or sale.

Use case:
A cleaner, lower-risk company usually supports a stronger valuation.
