This document is step 6 of the company sale readiness plan.

Its purpose is to keep Autobazar123 legally clean enough that a future buyer does not discover avoidable problems.

In simple words:

- the company should clearly own what it uses
- the company should clearly control what it depends on
- important decisions should be properly recorded

## Why This Matters

Many companies look good from the outside but become difficult to sell because basic ownership is messy.

Common problems:

- code written without IP assignment
- domains owned personally by a founder
- ad accounts tied to a personal email
- stock or option paperwork missing
- important contracts unsigned

These problems often do not stop the company from operating.

But they can absolutely slow down or weaken a sale.

## The Main Rule

Anything critical to the business should be clearly owned or controlled by the company.

That includes:

- code
- domains
- trademarks
- product designs
- databases
- hosting accounts
- analytics tools
- ad accounts
- payment tools
- major contracts

## What The Company Must Own

### 1. Code And Product IP

The company should own:

- source code
- internal tools
- designs
- copy created for the product
- logos and brand assets
- documentation

Required habit:

- every employee signs confidentiality and IP assignment
- every contractor signs confidentiality and IP assignment before work starts

Why:
If ownership is unclear, a buyer may treat the product itself as legally risky.

### 2. Domains And Brand Assets

The company should control:

- main domain
- parked or defensive domains
- email domain settings
- trademarks if registered
- logo source files

Why:
If the founder personally owns the domain or brand assets, the company is weaker and transfer becomes messy.

### 3. Infrastructure And SaaS Accounts

The company should control:

- hosting
- cloud storage
- CDN
- database services
- analytics tools
- CRM
- error tracking
- payment providers
- social media and ad platforms

Recommended habit:

- use company email identities
- avoid founder-only access
- document who has admin access

Why:
This is basic transferability.

### 4. Customer And Partner Contracts

Important contracts should be:

- written
- signed
- stored in one place
- easy to find

Examples:

- dealer contracts
- enterprise deals
- ad partnerships
- agency agreements
- major vendor contracts

Why:
During diligence, buyers want to know what obligations and rights they are inheriting.

## Basic Legal Hygiene Checklist

These are the basic records the company should keep organized.

### Corporate Records

Keep:

- incorporation records
- bylaws or equivalent governing documents
- shareholder approvals
- board approvals
- stock issuances
- option grants

Why:
These show that the company was properly formed and properly governed.

### People Records

Keep:

- employment agreements
- contractor agreements
- confidentiality agreements
- IP assignment agreements
- compensation summaries

Why:
People create value, but they also create ownership and compliance risk if paperwork is missing.

### Contract Records

Keep:

- standard templates
- signed agreements
- renewal dates
- termination rights
- exclusivity terms

Why:
This helps the company understand obligations and gives buyers confidence.

## Account Ownership Rules

These rules are simple and important.

### Use Company Emails

Important accounts should be created with company-controlled emails, not personal ones.

Why:
This makes access and transfer much easier.

### Avoid Single-Admin Risk

Important systems should not depend on one founder account.

Why:
If one account is lost, locked, or leaves, the company should still function.

### Keep An Admin Access List

Maintain a simple list of:

- system name
- admin users
- billing owner
- recovery owner

Why:
This reduces hidden operational fragility.

### Offboard Cleanly

When someone leaves:

- remove access
- rotate critical credentials if needed
- update ownership lists

Why:
Access hygiene matters for both security and diligence.

## IP Assignment In Plain Language

IP assignment means:

- work created for the company belongs to the company

This is especially important for:

- developers
- designers
- agencies
- freelancers

Use case:
If a freelance developer builds a key feature but never assigns the IP, the company may not fully own that feature.

## Founders' Personal Accounts To Remove Over Time

These are common founder traps:

- personal domain registrar account
- personal Google account for analytics
- personal Stripe or payment control
- personal Meta or Google Ads ownership
- personal cloud billing account

The goal is not to shame early shortcuts.

The goal is to gradually remove them and make the company independent.

## Practical Monthly Legal Hygiene Habits

Every month:

- store newly signed agreements
- update admin access list
- review any new contractors
- record important approvals

Every quarter:

- review cap table accuracy
- review option grants
- review key contracts and renewals
- review who owns critical accounts

Every year:

- review trademark and brand asset ownership
- review contractor IP coverage
- review governance records for missing items

## What A Buyer Will Ask

A buyer will often want to know:

- Who owns the code?
- Do all employees and contractors assign IP?
- Which contracts are material?
- Are there exclusivity obligations?
- Are any key assets personally owned?
- Who must approve the sale?

If you can answer these quickly, the company feels much safer.

## Simple Summary

This step is about making sure the company really owns and controls its business.

That sounds boring, but it is one of the clearest ways to reduce acquisition risk.
